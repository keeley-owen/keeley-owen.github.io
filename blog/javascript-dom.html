Javascript (our capricious hero) and their steadfast companion, the DOM - A modern tale of adventure

Today I shall recant the illustrious feats of sheer brilliance that the DOM is capable of, and the confusing, bewildering, utterly mystifying capabilities of Javascript.

Javascript and its Relationship to HTML & CSS:

For our first tale, we will learn more about JavaScript and how it interacts with HTML & CSS. Now, we can think of JavaScript as the squad commander, HTML as our battle-hardened soldiers, and CSS as their glorious banner. Each has a part to play, and how they define themselves and their part to play is key.

HTML, our army, forms the foundations of our crusade, structuring the very essence of our webpage. CSS, our banner, allows our soldiers to display flair and style, making them easily recognizable to friends and foes alike. It showcases our unique identity and coat of arms, represented by font and styling elements.

JavaScript, our illustrious commander, stands at the helm, orchestrating the ebs and flow of combat that is our webpage interactivity. It possesses the ability to dynamically respond to various situations and user actions, akin to a battlefield general countering enemy advances with grace and tact that befits our glorious leader.

Control Flow & Loops:

Now, we move on to Control Flow, which is like infiltrating enemy territory. You'll need to chart a course and adapt to changing conditions as more intel becomes available. JavaScript accomplishes this by following a predetermined path but can make decisions based on conditions that you control. An example of this is having a baseline mission structure and an outline of routine paths and advances. Differing conditions will mean the approach changes, like when you're in a swamp or a desert; your routine must adapt accordingly, this is Control Flow.

Loops are next, and they are far easier to explain. Loops are steeped in the repetition of a task and can be thought of as practicing battle maneuvers. You practice these through repetition until a desired outcome is reached. You may need to practice loops a small or large number of times until you reach your objective. It is a fundamental part of JavaScript and allows us to easily get data, or tasks as needed.

An example of these from everyday life (which was the original question parameter I glossed over in my excitement to make everything battle related) would be a Monday routine performed in order every Monday; The usual flow of your monday is, wake up, reminisce about being asleep mere moments ago, make a coffee, quietly experience dread, have a shower, pontificate, get dressed, spin around in a circle four times, and then crack on with your day. Now lets say you wake up absolutely manic, and you have the energy of one thousand suns, with this condition you may choose to replace your moments of respite between tasks with shouting "WOO!", this is possible with Control Flow. Or the opposite of this, you may wake up with the enthusiasim of a tortoise, and choose to skip all of these tasks instead, this is also Control Flow.

The DOM (Document Object Model):

The DOM is our next topic, and although it was exceptionally confusing at first, once you understand how to let it asist you, it becomes akin to a trusted vizier that will not betray you.

THe DOM allows you to reshape your code in browser, without affecting the source, and lets you freely test out and view where issues may be arising from, while providing important context for your webpage - It melds with Javascript in showing you the interactivity of your page, while bringing up where the issues are at the same time.

An example of where the DOM has come in handy for me, was I could not for the life of me understand why my webpage was not displaying correctly, as I had followed the instructions to a T and was certain there were no mistakes, feverishly looking over my code for anything out of the ordinary. As it turns out I could have just looked at the error showing in the console, and determine from there that I had not made a letter uppercase, instead of pouring over the whole sheet looking in every nook and cranny. This made me appreciate the DOM more than I could've imagined.

Accessing Data from Arrays and Objects:

Now, We shall cut the battle themes for the rest of the blog in order to express the content in a more succient fashion as we move onto Arrays & Objects, fitting these with acts of millitary might proved rather difficult...

Arrays are fantastic little buckets we can use to store information and they are all numbered neatly in a row, starting from 0. We can use an Javascript to get the contents of these vessels, which are numbered from left to right and all seperate. We may want to store this information logically, like arrange things based on alphabetical order or most important to least important, the array will not discriminate and simply numbers the vessel off accordingly based on placement. You may have a list of meats ['Beef', 'Chicken', 'Kereru', 'Duck', 'Pork', 'Bambi'] and you may want Duck, you would command Javascript to pull out meatbox 4 for Duck in this instance, however to get to the succulent Kereru you'll need to (politely) ask for meatbox 3 instead. Wow you've learnt so much from this!

Objects are up next, and they can be thought of as drawers, and they contain different information in each drawer that has its own little label (key). Next, to access one of these draws, you can ask Javascript to grab the information contained out ONLY after you have given it the label of said draw, for example if you have a Object called Ken the Kereru with the following information; {name: Ken, meat: Kereru, age: 38, taste: Delicious, sex: rarely} and you want it's name, you'd ask Javascript for the 'name' draw, if you want it's age you'd ask for the 'age' draw instead.

In summary, Arrays work off numbers from 0 upwards, and Objects work off labels (keys) to get the values. If you need a list of names an Array makes sense, if you need information that's organised via labels go with an Object instead.

Functions and Their Importance:

Finally, we have Functions! Which are super fun once you get the hang of them, and not at all confusing at first.

The nuts and bolts of Functions are they are reusable instructions in your code that can be called upon in your time of need, whenever that may be. Functions are given names, and they contain instructions for tasks that are called arguments, and we then call upon it by using its name.

These are very handy as it allows us to keep our code neat(ish) and make it easier to manage, since we can call upon the function multiple times at different points as well.
